Alan Turing's Life

1912 - 1954
Maida Vale, London



Alan Mathison Turing, one of the greatest minds of the 20th century, led an extraordinary life both during and after the war.
In World War II, Turing played a pivotal role in British intelligence, contributing to the renowned decryption project called "Ultra" at Bletchley Park.

There, Turing played a key role in developing the "Bombe", a mechanical device capable of deciphering encrypted messages used by Nazi Germany.
His groundbreaking work in cryptanalysis enabled the decryption of German Enigma codes, providing valuable military intelligence to the Allies.

Post-war, Turing delved into various scientific fields and pursued his passion for artificial intelligence.
He devised the famous "Turing machine," a theoretical model laying the foundation for modern computer science.
His research pioneered new paths in artificial intelligence and significantly influenced computer development.

However, Turing's life also bore a dark period.
In 1952, he was arrested due to his homosexuality, it was a criminal offense in the UK.
Subjected to hormone therapy (estrogen) as an alternative to prison, Turing endured immense suffering.
In 1954, at the age of 41, he died of cyanide poisoning (probably injected in an apple, the fruit he always eats) in an apparent suicide.

Only many years after his death was Turing's extraordinary legacy fully recognized.
In 2013, he received a posthumous royal pardon for his unjust criminal conviction.
Turing has been celebrated as a war hero and one of the founding fathers of modern computing.
His life and work have inspired generations of scientists and continue to have a profound impact on our society.
